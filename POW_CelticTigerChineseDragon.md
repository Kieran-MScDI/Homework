|It was in the company's DNA to establish a development centre in the heart of China. Bottom-<br/>line cost control was important, but in the ‘enterprise’ space revenue was king and the<br/>company already ran lean production teams in its western centres. The management team's<br/>traditional emphasis was on the top line; new sales, big deals, big customers, big-new-<br/>customers. They had achieved this in the past by being close to the customer. Now projects<br/>in China were beginning to dwarf those in European and American markets; Chinese semi-<br/>states and private corporations were investing heavily in IT infrastructure. In the end the<br/>decision was straightforward. Of all the developing markets China was the biggest and<br/>growing fastest.<br/> | |

Note:
Oshri et al., (2009) Chapter 10, Emerging Issues in Sourcing Strategy, content is redistributed in the 4th edition (Oshri et al., 2023) as follows; Chapter 5 Supplier Configurations and Capabilities (for Bundling Services), Chapter 11 Captive and Shared Service Centres, Chapter 12 Innovation through Outsourcing).

*"the clustering of economic activity, which is predominantly city based, is linked to the reorganization of international companies pursuing new markets, and this spatial reconfiguration means many women turn to home-based employment or telework. These informal employment activities provide key sources of income and hence degrees of independence, but they supply capitalism with flexible, extremely cheap, and unprotected labor."* (Gillard et al., 2008, p. 263, para. 3) <br/>
Written in 2008 I wonder if the the shift to remote working has helped to balance the scales in women's favour, and if it has will the progress made be washed away by current strategies to pull workers back into the office.

"*At the heart of low-cost production for global markets is “flexible production” and women are seen as the most flexible labor supply in terms of their willingness to accept temporary contracts and unstable working conditions.*" (Gillard et al., 2008, p. 268, para. 4) <br/> 
I think this is an important point. There is still, at least in my case, an expectation that men should provide. This is not just an external expectation but also comes from an internal feeling of that is where men's value lies. I am sure there are many women who will also have a built-in feeling that their value is in providing care for the family. Yet the opposite has been proven to work; women stepping up in World War 2 to take on full time industrial work, more recently 'stay at home' dads and highly successful women in business, that is to say **published** accounts of successful women; there are many historical accounts of women who are only recognised today for their work with some interesting accounts from Helen Lewis (2025). But our mindset tends towards so called traditional roles which brings me back to a passage earlier in the paper about Social Construction:<br/>
"*Individuals are born and immediately classified as female or male, and over time acquire a gendered identity, that is, what it means to be feminine or masculine (Elson, 1995). Both gendered concepts are relational (Wajcman, 1998), that is, they are construed in relation to each other by defining what the female is not in relation to the Western masculine norm; this varies from one environment to another and shifts over time.*" (Gillard et al., 2008, p. 264, para. 5) <br/>
The challenge with changing Social Construction is that it takes time, maybe a generation, maybe longer, but it does need constant attention, research, and discourse.

"*Underlining the assembly-line nature of the work, employers require flexible working with shifts, which frequently prevents women with families from taking jobs during off-peak hours. So while globalization may have brought new opportunities to young women with familiarity in English in the new service sector jobs, it has imposed homogeneous and culturally specific work practices and made large numbers of older women redundant in the manufacturing sector.*" (Gillard et al., 2008, p. 271, para. 1) <br/>
"*Women may have made notable progress into highly skilled work such as software programming in Brazil, India, and Malaysia, yet nowhere are these jobs held by a majority of women;womenworkingintheseareas comprise a small, educated elite (Hafkin & Taggart, 2001)*" (Gillard et al., 2008, p. 271, para. 2) <br/>
Are we seeing this imbalance as a result of failing to attract women into engineering/technical education at the outset? Even if so it does not explain the disparity in salaries when women do take up these roles.

"*CCNA program managers, instructors, and students found that employers were reluctant to recruit women, perceiving them as poorly qualified and lacking critical work experience.*"  (Gillard et al., 2008, p. 272, para. 1) <br/>
From my own recent experience I found male candidates more likely to enhance their skills or bluff answering a technical question, female candidates less so and prepared to respond with "I don't know". Honesty, not technical excellence, was key to the successful candidate taking up the role.<br/><br/>

Overall the Gillard et al. paper (2008) reveals a complex relationship between culture, globalisation, and women's recognition for the roles they play. The impact on pay and opportunities is particularly disturbing and demands action by organisations to ensure they are not contributing to the inequalities described, on the contrary they should be working to eliminate them.
