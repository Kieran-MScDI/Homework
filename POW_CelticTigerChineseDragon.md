# Celtic Tiger, Chinese Dragon (case)

|Case | Comments|
|:--- |:---------------------------- |
|It was in the company's DNA to establish a development centre in the heart of China. Bottom-<br/>line cost control was important, but in the ‘enterprise’ space revenue was king and the<br/>company already ran lean production teams in its western centres. The management team's<br/>traditional emphasis was on the top line; new sales, big deals, big customers, big-new-<br/>customers. They had achieved this in the past by being close to the customer. Now projects<br/>in China were beginning to dwarf those in European and American markets; Chinese semi-<br/>states and private corporations were investing heavily in IT infrastructure. In the end the<br/>decision was straightforward. Of all the developing markets China was the biggest and<br/>growing fastest.<br/> | |
|**Excerpts from interviews with a Senior Software Engineer (Chinese) from another<br/>company and the Director of Product Management (Beijing).**<br/> **(Director of Product Management)** <br/>"Establishing a development centre in Beijing was a dream project in many ways. For a<br/>merger or acquisition you have to work 'after the fact' with a pre-established team. In Beijing it<br/>was a clean slate, new hires, using our 'foreign' methodology."<br/>"...one of the reasons was it [the Beijing office] added value to the company. If we think about<br/>the four of five possible strategies for a mid-tier tech company, well, having a foothold in<br/>China - high profile, high value projects - it ticks all the boxes."<br/>"When you set up an office in China or India, there's a fear among people at the home base!<br/>You immediately create the potential for a social divide that applies when you move to an<br/>offshore site."<br/> | | 
|"Do you want a situation where you say 'designed in California, made in China'? What does<br/>that say about a social divide in the company? That's the choice you have to make."<br/>"Our core methodologies and practices are Scrum/Agile so we decided to use Scrum from the<br/>start. The local engineers looked on it as a 'foreign' methodology, but no resistance and open<br/>to learning about it."<br/> | | 
|"My view is that Agile is a strict discipline; it's more disciplined that other methods if you do it<br/>in a highly structured way, get rid of the 'ad hoc' style that seems to be associated with it.<br/>Start your stand-ups at 10am (not 5 past 10), finish strictly at 10:15am. The stand-up<br/>conversation should be structured; specific challenges approaching, specific problems you<br/>are facing, you've either run or written a test case. Programming's like that, there's 'do or<br/>don't', 'did or didn't', no ambiguity and you need these type of conversations, Agile brings this<br/>to the front. But I was worried Agile could create problems with 'face', engineer's admitting to<br/>difficulty or failing to achieve something."<br/> | | 
|**(Senior Engineer)** <br/>"In China software engineering career has quite a lot of respect. In general, software<br/>engineers work long hours, often seven days a week, starting early, leaving late. International<br/>companies might have more formal policies, leave work at 7pm, come back in the morning.<br/>But not as flexible as smaller companies, usually working around a project, if an emergency<br/>comes in they might have to work all night."<br/> | | 
|"Now Agile and Scrum are well known in China. In the real implementation there are some<br/>cultural differences that might make it feel different to an implementation in the US."<br/>"Let me put it this way; In western countries, people like to treat work like playing games. A<br/>manager here is something like the parent, not the children. People don't accept the childish<br/>approach. Engineers think 'we are not kids, we are adults'. So Agile can inspire in the West<br/>because it is playful, but in China?..."<br/> | |
